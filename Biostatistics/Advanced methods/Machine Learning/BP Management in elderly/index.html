BP Management in Elderly

Weber Liu BSc(Adv) MD MSc(ClinEpid)

Background

Hypertension is a disease of the elderly, rising in prevalence and incidence globally as a result of both the obesity epidemic and changing population structures. The prevalence of hypertension increases with age, with only 27% in people younger than 60 years, but 74% in people aged 80 and older.1 There is uncertainty about how hypertension should be best managed in older people, especially in elderly people with multimorbidity or frailty. Current guidelines provide limited recommendations regarding treatment of hypertension in elderly people.2 The heterogeneity in comorbidity, frailty, polypharmacy, and cognitive impairment makes the treatment of hypertension in older people challenging. While there is substantial evidence that BP lowering in the elderly reduces cardiovascular events, the uncertainty is regarding how to apply this evidence – specifically about the balance between safety and efficacy as people become frail and having multimorbidity. Clinicians often suggest caution with BP lowering among older adults and some guidelines have suggested caution or relaxing of BP targets among frail older people.3

According to the 2018 Guidelines for the management of arterial hypertension of the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) and the European Society of Hypertension (ESH), in patients aged 65-80 the target systolic blood pressure (SBP) is 130-139 mmHg.3 For patients aged >80 years, an SBP target range of 130–139 mmHg is also recommended if tolerated. For initiation of drug treatment, it is recommended to initiate antihypertensive treatment with a two-drug combination, preferably in a single pill combination. The exceptions are frail older patients and those at low risk and with grade 1 hypertension (particularly if SBP is <150 mmHg).3 Most cohort studies and clinical trials focusing on BP targets included patients that were already receiving antihypertension.4 There is limited evidence on antihypertensive treatment in older patients with newly diagnosed hypertension. This epidemiological study examines difference in blood pressure at the time of diagnosis of hypertension between elderly and young patients in the Australian population. Further we wish to elucidate the class and number of antihypertensives initiated in these differences and examine any age-related differences. Finally, we compare periods of effective BP control, defined as a period of no further hypertensive measurements following initiation.

This study follows the STROBE Statement for reporting of retrospective cohort

    • Reason: as per NICE guidelines NG136 section 1.2.2.

  6. Where no hypertension diagnosis date can be attributed, the patient is removed
